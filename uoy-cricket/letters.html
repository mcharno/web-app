   Here’s the first email from Jim…………………

 

    Dear Boys,

     what is the rule about the oak (?) tree inside the boundary on 22 acres?

        If the ball hits it, does it have to hit the trunk or what?

      And how many runs does the umpire signal?

 

 

  Answer.

       Dear Jim,

             These rules are generally known as 'local rules' and apply to any sort of natural obstruction, trees, streams, sheds etc.

                 In our case you are right it is an Oak tree and the rule is that if the ball having been struck by a batsman hits any part of the tree then it is 4 runs. The ball is also dead once it hits the tree so you can't catch it bouncing off the trunk. Sometimes this can work in the batsman's favour where the ball might hit the tree before a fielder can stop it crossing the boundary, but it can also work against the batsman as in the famous incident in a game between Chemistry and Computer Science. Chemistry needed 6 to win off the last ball to be bowled by Jim Briggs. Simon Duckett hit the ball which was going for 6 runs until it clipped a branch on its way over the boundary line. Chemistry began applauding the winning hit only to have it pointed out, by Jim, that since the ball had hit the tree it was only 4 runs!

         We have also generally ruled that it is NOT 4 runs if a fielder clips a branch with the ball whilsr throwing it back to the wicket keeper. However, were he to throw it against the trunk and the ball to bounce over the line then that would be 4 runs.  (I think).
